Concept: Two Pointers

This is one of the most important DSA techniques.

Many interview questions use it.

First Understand the Problem

Suppose:

arr = [10,20,30,40,50]

We want to reverse it.

You already know:

arr[::-1]

But interviewers want logic.

Human Thinking

Current array:

10 20 30 40 50
 ↑           ↑
left       right

Swap:

50 20 30 40 10

Move pointers:

50 20 30 40 10
    ↑     ↑
  left right

Swap again:

50 40 30 20 10

Now:

50 40 30 20 10
       ↑

Done.

What Are Two Pointers?

Two variables:

left = 0
right = len(arr)-1

One starts from beginning.

One starts from end.

They move toward each other.

Visual Example

Array:

[10,20,30,40,50]

Initially:

left = 0
right = 4

10 20 30 40 50
↑           ↑

Swap:

50 20 30 40 10

Move:

left += 1
right -= 1

Now:

50 20 30 40 10
   ↑     ↑

Swap:

50 40 30 20 10

Done.

Algorithm
Step 1:
Initialize left = 0

Step 2:
Initialize right = n-1

Step 3:
While left < right

Step 4:
Swap arr[left] and arr[right]

Step 5:
left += 1

Step 6:
right -= 1

Step 7:
Return array
Code
def reverse_array(arr):

    left = 0
    right = len(arr)-1

    while left < right:

        arr[left], arr[right] = arr[right], arr[left]

        left += 1
        right -= 1

    return arr
Why Is This Important?

This technique is used in:

✅ Reverse Array

✅ Palindrome Check

✅ Remove Duplicates

✅ Move Zeroes

✅ Sorted Array Problems

✅ Pair Sum Problems

Many interview questions are just variations of Two Pointers.

Day 13 Questions
Q1

Use Two Pointers to reverse:

[10,20,30,40,50]

Don't use:

[::-1]
Q2

Check if:

"madam"

is a palindrome using Two Pointers.

Hint:

left = 0
right = len(word)-1
Q3

Check if:

"python"

is a palindrome.

Expected:

False
Q4

Count how many swaps are required to reverse:

[1,2,3,4,5,6]

(Hint: increment a swap counter)

Q5 ⭐

Reverse only the string:

"akshatra"

using Two Pointers.

No slicing.

New Learning Habit

For every question, write:

Dry Run:

left = ?
right = ?

Array becomes?

left moves?
right moves?
The ability to dry-run code is what separates beginners from strong DSA problem solvers.
